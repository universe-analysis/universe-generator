Do jammed groups braid like Bilson-Thompson fermions? A B₃ census of unique groups

Analysis of the stored CONVERGE 2+1 subpath dumps (no new campaign) · analysis module + tests 0c9f1dc · census figure 83bbf26

LQG's preon program (Bilson-Thompson, hep-ph/0503213; with Markopoulou & Smolin, hep-th/0603022) builds every first-generation Standard Model fermion from one 3-strand braiding template — the minimal 2-crossing braid σ₁σ₂⁻¹ or its mirror — with charge and color carried by ribbon twists. Our unique groups are clusters of worldlines that touch each other and nothing else, and in 2+1 (exactly where point-strand braiding is topologically meaningful; in 3+1 it is trivial without framing) each n-strand group traces a genuine element of the braid group Bn. New analysis/analyze_braids.py extracts each group's braid word from the x-projection crossings of the comoving strands and classifies 3-strand groups by a complete B₃ invariant (Burau at t = −1 into SL(2,ℤ) plus exponent sum), over the 40 CONVERGE subpath dumps (T = 20–300, 5 seeds).

  • Touching means braiding. 49% of 2-strand groups are genuinely linked (nonzero signed crossing sum), 69% of 3-strand groups carry a nontrivial B₃ class, and by n ≈ 10 essentially every group is braided (≥95%). Packing contact is not incidental adjacency — the strands wind.
  • But the braids are hot, not minimal. A typical 3-strand group carries ~260 raw crossings (44M crossings across the census); its class is far beyond any short word. The Bilson-Thompson first-generation template appears exactly 3 times in 3,736 triples (0.08%) — consistent with random incidence, not selection. Jamming pressure does not preferentially generate minimal fermion braids; it generates deeply wound "molecules".
  • Even-dominance: the groups are mostly pure braids. The 2-strand crossing-sum distribution (b) alternates strongly — even sums dominate — meaning strands usually return to their original x-order: closed-loop worldlines pinned at the Bang/Crunch favor pure (permutation-free) braids.
  • Chirality is balanced. Sign of the exponent sum: pairs +2323/−2191, triples +1210/−1166 — no parity violation beyond ~2σ; the packing does not select a handedness.
  • Resolution caveat. 31% of crossings are "fragile" (strand separation at the crossing below the engine's 2/T contact scale), so individual deep classes are provisional even though crossing-count parities and linking sums are robust. Only 87 triples are fragile-free, and 99% of those are trivial: robust braiding and sub-resolution contact go together.

This is inherently a 2+1-only chart — the 3+1 twin cannot exist for point strands (braids untie in three spatial dimensions; that is precisely why Bilson-Thompson uses framed ribbons, whose twist our worldlines cannot carry). The honest conclusion: packing produces abundant, chirality-balanced, mostly-pure braiding, but no preference for the Standard Model's minimal braid classes. If a correspondence exists it would need either a framing (a ribbon structure from the worldline geometry, e.g. tangent-turning) or a coarse-graining that cancels the thermal crossings — both open follow-ups.

Nobody beats the blue line: multi-term phase combinations stay under the single-term reach envelope

Numerical stress test (no campaign) · analysis module + tests 2876484

Kevin asked whether the 07-16 reach chart really covered all possible phases — and, fairly, wanted data rather than just the linearity argument for the multi-term case. The claim under test: with the unit rapidity budget Σ|a·b| = 1 split across k terms, no choice of frequencies, budget shares, amplitude signs, or phases can out-reach the phased single-term envelope (the blue line) at any time. Two attacks: a 200,000-config random sweep per k ∈ {2, 3, 4} drawn from the actual sampling ensemble (distinct integer frequencies, Dirichlet budget splits, f ~ U[0, 2π) on even frequencies, f ∈ {0, π} on odd), and a Nelder-Mead adversary at 25 fixed times, free to pick any signed budget split and any admissible phases over frequency sets seeded with the top single-term performers.

  • The optimizer converges to the envelope exactly (ratio 1 at every probed time, worst excess +2.2×10−16 — machine epsilon, never above): its winning move is always to concentrate the whole budget on the best single frequency at its optimal phase — it rediscovers the linearity argument numerically. At π/2 the 2-term adversary lands on √2, the b = 2, f = π/4 optimum, to 1e-4.
  • Random ensemble configs approach from below and never cross: best sweep ratios 0.99990 (k = 2), 0.99825 (k = 3), 0.98912 (k = 4). More terms reach less — each extra term dilutes the budget, exactly as Σwiri ≤ max ri predicts.
  • Phase coverage is exact, not sampled. The displacement is linear in (sin f, cos f), so the envelope's hypot(Δα, Δβ) is the analytic maximum over the whole continuous phase circle; the ensemble reaches all of it because f ~ U[0, π) plus the ± amplitude sign covers [0, 2π) (a → −a ≡ f → f + π), and odd frequencies admit only f ∈ {0, π} (loop closure), both covered by the |Δβ| branch.

Locked in tests/test_multiterm_reach.py: the random sweep never exceeds the envelope, the optimizer saturates at it, and the π/2 optimum is √2.

What the tried functions actually look like: example 2-term worldlines and the split-linearity line

Follow-up to the entry above: Kevin wanted to see concrete examples of the functions the sweep tried, to make sure the budget really is being split across terms. Every sampled config is a genuine worldline x(z) = Σi ai[sin(biz + fi) − sin fi] with amplitudes ai = ±wi/bi, so the rapidity budget Σ|aibi| = Σwi = 1 exactly (verified to 1e-12 on every example). The four shown are the two best free draws at z = π/2 plus the two best genuinely split draws (every term forced to hold ≥ 30% of the budget):

  • x(z) = −6.3e−5 [sin(170z + 2.93) − sin 2.93] + 0.495 [sin(2z + 0.88) − sin 0.88] — disp 1.4031 (0.992 of √2). The best free draw self-concentrates: 99% of the budget on b = 2 near the optimal phase π/4, a whisker of budget wasted on b = 170.
  • x(z) = −0.00314 sin(109z) + 0.329 [sin(2z + 3.80) − sin 3.80] — a real 34/66 split (odd b = 109 carries 0.00314×109 = 0.34 of the budget); disp 1.2622, only 0.89 of the envelope.
  • x(z) = 0.202 [sin(3z + 3.14) − sin 3.14] + … — the odd-frequency phases show up only as f ∈ {0, π} (here b = 3 at f = π, b = 7 at f = 0), the loop-closure constraint; a 60/40 split reaching 0.89 of the envelope.

The right panel is the whole argument made visible: for any frequency pair, the phase-maximized reach is exactly linear in the budget split (numeric dots on the predicted line to 2e-5, locked in tests/test_multiterm_reach.py), so the maximum over splits always sits at an endpoint — concentration. Splitting with b = 2 + 4 at w = 0 shows the even partner's reach 1.0 exactly (b = 4's own phased optimum at π/2); no interior w ever pokes above the √2 dashed line.
