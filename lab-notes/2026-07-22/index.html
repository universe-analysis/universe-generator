Partners share a hidden anchor: selection correlations in unique groups, separated from dynamical contact

Analysis of the stored CONVERGE 2+1 subpath dumps (no new campaign) · analysis module + tests dcc6939

Kevin asked whether co-grouped paths — which emerge from different comoving regions and only meet later to satisfy the subpath intersection requirement — amount to "quantum entanglement sort of stuff". The model is a global self-consistency selection over whole histories, so co-grouped strands can carry correlations that no slice-by-slice evolution would explain; this entry quantifies that, as classical groundwork for the README's stated goal of Bell-type tests on subpaths. Strands emerge at X(0) = Σ a·b·cos f + a₁ per axis (the z → 0 limit of x/sin z; with the exact unit budget |a·b| = 1 this is a₁ ± 1 on odd axes, a₁ + cos f on even ones), and first contact is computed with the engine's own predicate — wrapped Chebyshev distance ≤ 2/T on the engine z grid — validated by recovering the admission-guaranteed contact for 9,246 of 9,246 two-strand groups. Nulls are within-dump partner reshuffles (single-strand marginals preserved, co-membership destroyed; features dump-centered), over 40 dumps, 461,917 strands, 59,135 groups (2,359 giant >10-strand groups excluded from pairwise work).

  • Partners share a hidden anchor. The free comoving coordinate a₁ correlates at r = +0.95 between the two strands of a 2-group (wrapped anchor gap 0.019 vs 0.498 for reshuffled pairs — 26× tighter, z = −224). Meanwhile their visible Bang positions sit a mean 0.33 apart (a third of the half-box, vs null 0.67): partners genuinely emerge from different places while agreeing almost exactly on the timeless parameter that anchors their mid-loop trajectory.
  • Correlation precedes contact. Contact comes mostly early (median z/π = 0.10, in the endpoint-stretched era), but 242 pairs (2.6%) first touch after the turnaround — and they still carry r = +0.74 anchor correlation (wrapped gap 0.117 vs 0.450 null). Pairs that spent the entire expanding half of the universe without any interaction were already matched at the Bang. Never-touching co-group siblings (6,973 pairs from 3–10-strand groups) correlate at r = +0.90 with no direct contact ever — correlation mediated purely by the shared group.
  • The class variable anti-matches. Partners avoid being alike in the cold/mover classification: same-class rate 47% vs 54% null in 2-groups, collapsing to 19% vs 47% in the late-contact ensemble, which contains essentially no cold+cold pairs. (Two cold strands with matched anchors co-emerge exactly — odd-axis displacement is ±1 mod 2 — so they either touch immediately or never: surviving late rendezvous requires one mover.) A discrete partner property that is anti-correlated under selection is the closest structural analog here to anticorrelated spins.
  • The rendezvous is not kinematically arranged. Even-axis phase alignment and the approaching-at-Bang rate are both null — partners do not aim at each other; the correlation lives in the selection, not the initial velocities.
  • Interpretation, kept honest. The anchor-sharing is plausibly enforced by exclusivity, not by the meeting itself: a subpath must touch exactly one group, and a strand that strays from its parent's neighbourhood through the crowded epochs hits a foreign group and is rejected — a monogamy constraint. All of this is classical common-constraint correlation ("Bertlmann's socks with extra steps"): it reproduces the phenomenology that motivates all-at-once/retrocausal accounts of entanglement (correlation without prior interaction, no signalling, set by a global constraint), but a genuine Bell test needs measurement settings and would, in this class of model, run through the measurement-independence loophole. The quantitative surprise is the magnitude: a 26× anchor tightening and a class anti-correlation, both invisible to any single-slice observer.
  • Scope. 2+1 only — the 3+1 engine has no --subpaths mode yet, so the dimension twin waits on an engine port (queued).
